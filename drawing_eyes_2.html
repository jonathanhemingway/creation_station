Lights and Shadows

Bringing it to Life

Publushed September 20, 2017

Have you ever wondered how drawings can look so real? How they just leap off of the page and stare you in the face? All of that is in the light and shadow applied to the drawing. Line work is great because it gives drawings structure and order. It makes sure that everything is exactly where it is supposed to be. However, it’s only half of the picture. Using shadows and different shading techniques will give your drawing that third dimension and make it pop off of the page. It will bring it to life.

What You'll Need

  • Pencils
  • Eraser
  • Paper
  • Flat Surface
  • Knowledge of Shading

1

Look at different images of eyes. You should see that there is always a source of light that is reflecting off of the surface of the eye itself (as seen on the iris and sometimes the pupil). Sometimes times, there is also a shadow on the sclera (white) of the eye. Depending on the texture of the subject’s skin and the lighting in the image, there are various shadows around the eye.

2

Begin identifying parts of the eye that have shadow by adding light amounts of shade. As you study, gradually add shade to parts that are darker and darker until you get to the darkest portions of the image. The darkest portion would typically be the iris as it is almost pitch black. Depending on the image you are referencing, areas around the eye (like wrinkles or the corner towards the nose) might also have heavy shadows.

3

Begin sketching the eyelashes, top and bottom lids, and the fold at the top of the eyelid using shading techniques. Remember that although the eyelashes spring out of the eyelids, they don’t look like spider limbs. They flow in a more curved direction. Also, remember that the eyelids aren’t necessarily lines, but more so different sides of one thing, which can be represented well with shading.

4

Practice, practice, and practice. Look up different images to reference using other kinds of shading techniques. Try to find a diverse set of images that range from low, medium, and, high amounts of contrast.

Extra Notes

  • When looking for reference images to draw from, try to find black and white images. It will be easier to identify the light source and shadows in those because there isn’t any color throwing you off.

  • To get the full effect of this exercise, it would be best if you are familiar with different kinds of shading techniques. These techniques include hatching, cross-hatching, stippling and line weight.

  • The best way to get better at drawing eyelashes is to practice drawing them. They aren’t to be drawn precisely, however, that also doesn’t mean that imaginary. They are just subjective.

  • Different types of graphite pencils are also great for shading and adding weight. The softer the graphite, the darker the pencil stroke is. This will allow for a more dynamic contrast between light and dark spaces.
